1 Introduction

This project analyzes a bank loan default dataset to explore factors associated with borrower default risk. The Tableau dashboard and story point provide an interactive visualization of borrower characteristics, loan types, credit score patterns, and default behavior.

2 Data Sources and Description

The dataset used in this project is the Bank Loan Default Dataset, which contains information on borrower characteristics, loan attributes, and loan default outcomes. The dataset consists of 1,000 observations and includes variables such as credit score, loan amount, employment status, marital status, loan type indicators, checking account balance, savings account balance, and loan default status.

The response variable is Default, where 1 indicates a borrower who defaulted on a loan and 0 indicates a borrower who did not default. Several explanatory variables were used to investigate factors associated with default risk, including credit score, marital status, loan amount, and loan type.

To support the analysis, a derived variable called Credit Score Group was created by categorizing credit scores into Low, Medium, and High groups. This transformation enabled a clearer comparison of default rates across different credit risk categories and provided more interpretable insights for storytelling and dashboard visualization.

3 Narrative of the Story

This project explores factors associated with loan default risk using a bank loan dataset. The analysis begins with an overview of borrower characteristics, including credit score distribution and loan type distribution. Most borrowers have credit scores concentrated between 700 and 850, while personal loans represent the largest loan category.

The story then examines the relationship between credit score and loan default risk. The results show a strong association between credit score and default behavior. Borrowers with low credit scores exhibit a default rate of 91.0%, compared with 46.0% for medium credit score borrowers and only 14.4% for high credit score borrowers. This finding suggests that credit score is one of the strongest indicators of loan default risk.

Next, the analysis explores the relationship between loan amount and credit score using a scatter plot. The visualization shows that borrowers with lower credit scores are more frequently associated with default cases, while borrowers with higher credit scores are concentrated in the non-default group.

Finally, marital status is examined as an additional borrower characteristic. Single borrowers have a higher default rate (34.1%) than married borrowers (26.6%), suggesting that borrower demographics may also provide useful information when assessing repayment risk.

The analysis demonstrates that credit score plays the most important role in predicting loan default risk, while borrower characteristics and loan attributes provide additional context for risk assessment.

6 Key Findings

The dashboard suggests that credit score is strongly associated with loan default risk. Borrowers with low credit scores have a much higher default rate than borrowers with medium or high credit scores. Single borrowers also show a higher default rate than married borrowers. In addition, personal loans are the most common loan type in the dataset.

7 Recommendations

Based on the dashboard analysis, credit score appears to be the strongest predictor of loan default risk. Borrowers in the low credit score group exhibit a substantially higher default rate (91.0%) compared with medium (46.0%) and high credit score borrowers (14.4%). Therefore, financial institutions should prioritize credit score as a key factor during the loan approval and risk assessment process.

For borrowers with low credit scores, lenders may consider implementing stricter lending requirements, such as lower loan limits, higher collateral requirements, or enhanced credit verification procedures. Additional monitoring and financial counseling programs may also help reduce the likelihood of default among high-risk borrowers.

The analysis also indicates that single borrowers have a higher default rate (34.1%) than married borrowers (26.6%). While marital status alone should not determine lending decisions, it may provide useful context when combined with other risk indicators. Lenders could incorporate demographic and behavioral factors into a broader risk assessment framework to improve predictive accuracy.

In addition, personal loans represent the largest loan category in the dataset. Financial institutions should pay particular attention to personal loan portfolios and regularly monitor borrower repayment behavior to identify potential risks early.

In conclusion, lenders should adopt a comprehensive risk management strategy that combines credit score, borrower characteristics, and loan attributes to improve loan performance and reduce default risk.

8 Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the results. First, the dataset contains only a limited set of borrower characteristics, and other potentially important factors such as income, employment stability, debt-to-income ratio, and repayment history are not available. Second, the analysis is exploratory and does not establish causal relationships between borrower characteristics and loan default behavior. Finally, the findings are based on a single dataset and may not generalize to all lending environments or borrower populations.

Future studies could incorporate additional financial variables and predictive modeling techniques to improve the understanding of loan default risk and support more accurate lending decisions.

9 Conclusion

This Tableau dashboard and story point demonstrate how interactive data visualization can support loan default risk analysis. The results suggest that credit score is an important factor in identifying default risk, while borrower characteristics such as marital status and loan type provide additional context for understanding repayment behavior.
